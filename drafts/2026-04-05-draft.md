you don't get to call my culture barbaric (but maybe i do)

Every Easter Monday, Czech men braid fresh willow twigs into a whip and go door to door, lightly whipping women on their legs and backside. The women say thank you, hand over a painted egg, and pour the men a shot of slivovice. If the men show up late, the women dump a bucket of cold water on them. Everyone laughs. Allegedly.

This is called pomlazka, and it's been happening in my country since at least the 14th century. The word comes from *pomladit*, meaning "to make younger." The idea is that fresh spring branches transfer vitality and fertility to whoever gets whipped. It has nothing to do with Jesus. It has everything to do with the same pagan logic that had Roman men at Lupercalia running around half-naked, hitting women with leather thongs for good luck.

I grew up with this. I have memories of Easter Mondays spent dodging cousins in my grandmother's garden, the smell of fresh-cut willow mixing with kolache dough from the kitchen, hiding behind the sofa while my uncle pretended he couldn't see me. The whip was always decorated with ribbons, each one added by a different girl he'd visited that morning. A heavily ribboned whip was a badge of honor. I remember thinking it was fun. My legs stung for about ten seconds and then I got chocolate.

It was fun. It was also, now that I think about it, an annual ritual where boys learned they had permission to chase girls, and girls learned that being chased was their role in the story. My grandmother wore three skirts layered on top of each other for cushioning. She'd been doing it since she was a child. She never questioned it, and for most of my life, neither did I.

Eighty percent of Czech women dislike the tradition. Forty-four percent report experiencing pain. Twenty-five percent report bruises. Nineteen percent report feeling helpless or humiliated. And yet 64% of Gen Z men support it, mostly because it's "a social event" (read: an excuse to drink slivovice at 10am on a Monday, which, fair).

The people who enjoy pomlazka are rarely the ones being hit. Funny how that works.

## the moment you stop being a tourist and start being a judge

Here's where it gets complicated. Because if you're reading this from outside Czechia, you probably had one of two reactions just now. Either "what the hell, that's insane" or "oh, that's kind of charming in a weird old-world way." And both reactions tell us more about you than about the tradition itself.

I bring up pomlazka not because it's the most egregious example of a problematic cultural practice (it isn't, not by a long shot), but because it's mine. It belongs to me in a way that allows me to critique it without performing moral superiority from a comfortable distance. I can say "this is kind of messed up" while also saying "I get why it exists and what it means to the people who practice it." I can hold both things.

But what happens when the tradition isn't yours?

What happens when you're traveling through La Guajira in Colombia and you learn about the Wayuu people's encierro, a coming-of-age ritual where a girl, at her first period, is secluded from the world for months (sometimes years), her hair cut short, her diet restricted to herbal drinks, her only company the women of her family who teach her to weave, cook, mediate conflicts, and carry cultural knowledge?

Your gut reaction is probably horror. Isolation. Food deprivation. A child. And I won't pretend that my first reaction was any different.

I first heard about the Wayuu through David, who is Colombian. We were talking about Indigenous communities in La Guajira, the desert peninsula where the Wayuu have lived for centuries, and he mentioned the encierro casually, the way you mention something you grew up knowing about. My reaction was immediate and visceral: that sounds like imprisonment. His reaction was more complicated. He grew up in a country where 800,000 Wayuu people live their lives, and the encierro is one thread in an enormous, ancient tapestry that also includes some of the most stunning textile art in the Americas. The Wayuu mochila bag is the most exported handicraft in all of Colombia. According to their mythology, a spider called Walekeru taught the first Wayuu woman to weave. The craft is sacred. And the encierro is where you learn it.

But here's the thing though. The Wayuu are a matrilineal society. Women carry the family name. Inheritance passes through the female line. In 2005, male elders in one Wayuu community appointed their first female chieftain, and the results were so good they permanently committed to appointing only women leaders. The encierro exists within a framework of female power, not male control.

The Wayuu writer Estercilia Simanca, who never went through the encierro herself, pushes back against the Western framing. "The encierro is not confinement," she says. "It's a room for the girl." She also acknowledges that because she skipped it, she was called a *venado*, a deer (eternally childish and rebellious), and that had she gone through the ritual, "surely her voice and her words would be different, as she would have learned to keep quiet when the time was right."

Read that again. A woman who became an internationally recognized writer partly because she wasn't taught silence, defending the tradition that would have taught her silence. If that doesn't make your head spin, you're not paying attention.

And then there's the thing that most Western coverage conveniently leaves out: the real existential threat to Wayuu culture isn't the encierro. It's El Cerrejon, one of the largest open-pit coal mines in the world, operating right in Wayuu territory. It's forced displacement, water scarcity, and government neglect. The Fuerza de Mujeres Wayuu (Force of Wayuu Women), founded in 2006, isn't fighting against the encierro. They're fighting against mining companies destroying their land. But that story doesn't generate the same satisfying moral outrage, does it? It's much easier to gasp at a coming-of-age ritual than to interrogate the supply chain of the coal that heats your apartment.

## the playbook hasn't changed

So who gets to decide what's barbaric?

Historically, the answer has been: whoever has the bigger army. And I mean that literally.

The Declaration of Independence, the founding document of the nation that currently sets the cultural agenda for most of the planet, refers to Native Americans as "merciless Indian savages." The Spanish held an actual public debate in Valladolid in 1550 about whether Indigenous peoples were human (while the killing continued, naturally). Kipling's "The White Man's Burden" framed the colonization of the Philippines as a selfless, thankless duty imposed on the white race by divine destiny, describing the colonized as "half-devil and half-child." The Holy Roman Empire didn't just persecute Roma people, it literally allowed any citizen to murder any Roma person they encountered and paid bounties for their capture.

The playbook is always the same: identify a cultural difference, frame it as evidence of inferiority, use that framing to justify control, exclusion, or violence. The specific targets rotate (Native Americans, Africans, Roma, Jews, now Muslims), but the rhetorical structure is identical. And the cultural practices cited as proof of barbarism? They're whatever is convenient. Cannibalism. Nomadism. Religious dietary laws. The wrong kind of clothing. The wrong kind of prayer.

The Roma case hits particularly close to home for me, being European. While we love to point fingers at "barbaric" practices in faraway places, Roma people have faced continuous persecution on this continent since the 14th century. They were branded as criminals and vagabonds. Multiple European countries forcibly sterilized Roma women well into the 21st century. Between 250,000 and 500,000 Roma were murdered during the Holocaust, and it took until the 1980s for anyone to officially recognize them as victims of racial persecution. Three concentration camp survivors had to go on hunger strikes to get that recognition. And this happened here. In the heart of "civilized" Europe. Not in some distant land we can comfortably tut-tut about from our apartments in Prague or Berlin.

The philosopher Martha Nussbaum has a line that keeps rattling around in my head. She argues that relativists "imagine homogeneity where there is really diversity, agreement or submission where there is really contestation." In other words, when we say "their culture does X," we're pretending that everyone within that culture agrees, which erases the people (usually women, usually the most vulnerable) who are already fighting against these practices from the inside.

But the colonial version of the argument does the same thing in reverse. When we say "that culture is barbaric because of X," we're reducing millions of people to their most shocking practice and ignoring everything else. Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie called this "the danger of a single story." One practice becomes the only story.

## the question nobody wants to answer honestly

Okay, so condemning entire cultures based on specific practices is colonial. And excusing everything because "it's their culture" is intellectually lazy. Where does that leave us?

Honestly? Uncomfortable. Which is maybe exactly where we should be.

Karl Popper called it the paradox of tolerance: if a tolerant society extends unlimited tolerance to the intolerant, tolerance itself gets destroyed. But John Rawls added a crucial caveat: jumping too quickly to suppress what we perceive as intolerance can itself become a form of oppression. The instinct to "save" people from their own culture has a long, bloody history, and it doesn't usually end well for the people being "saved."

(I'm looking at you, every 19th-century European empire that colonized Africa to "civilize" it.)

There's also the hypocrisy problem, and this is the part where I have to implicate myself. Because the same Western culture that recoils at, say, female genital cutting in parts of Africa has normalized breast implants, labiaplasty, child beauty pageants, and an entire cosmetic surgery industry built on the premise that women's natural bodies aren't good enough. An academic paper I read while researching this piece argued that the debate about FGM is often stuck between moral relativism (we can't judge) and moral hypocrisy (we judge others while ignoring comparable practices in our own culture). I don't think those two things are the same. But I think the distance between them is shorter than most people would like to admit.

And this is where the Islam conversation comes in, because you can't write about "barbaric traditions" and Western moral judgment without addressing the elephant in the room. The conservative talking point that Islam is fundamentally incompatible with progressive values has become one of the most weaponized versions of this argument. Sam Harris calls it a "direct link between doctrine and terrorism." Douglas Murray says Europe needs "a bit less Islam." And polling data gets cherry-picked to paint 1.8 billion people as a monolith.

But Muslim scholars have been having internal debates about reform, gender, and governance for centuries. Amina Wadud pioneered feminist Quranic interpretation when "there was no such thing as Islamic feminism." Mustafa Akyol has traced how a "war of ideas" between rationalist and dogmatic schools of Islam was won by the traditionalists, creating perceptions of the religion as inherently rigid. Tunisia outlawed polygamy in 1956 and recognized marital rape in 2017. Indonesia, the world's largest Muslim-majority country, has women serving as Supreme Court justices.

As Maajid Nawaz (a former Islamist turned liberal reformer) put it in his dialogue with Harris: "Islam is not a religion of war or of peace. It's a religion. Scripture exists; human beings interpret it."

The "incompatibility" argument collapses the distinction between Islamism (a political ideology) and Islam (a religion), and it conveniently serves European right-wing populism by providing a civilized-sounding framework for what is often just anti-immigration anxiety wearing a turtleneck.

I live in a country where barely anyone goes to church (only 9% of Czechs plan to attend Easter services, according to a recent survey), where atheism is practically a national sport, and where the most common spiritual practice is getting drunk in a pub and arguing about politics. And yet somehow, when the conversation turns to Islam, people who haven't opened a Bible since their christening suddenly become passionate defenders of "Christian values" and "Western civilization." Forgive me if I'm skeptical about the sincerity of that concern.

(Or maybe that's just Prague. But I doubt it.)

## so what do i actually think?

I think Kwame Anthony Appiah got closest to something useful when he proposed cosmopolitanism as a middle path: we have obligations to others beyond our own tribe, and we should take seriously the value of different ways of life, but cultural differences deserve respect only "in so far as they are not harmful to people."

The catch, obviously, is defining "harmful." And who gets to define it. And whether the people inside the culture agree with your definition. And whether your concern is genuine or just another flavor of "the white man's burden" dressed up in 21st-century progressive language.

I don't have a clean answer. I'm suspicious of anyone who does.

What I do have is a few things I keep coming back to when I'm traveling and I encounter something that makes me uncomfortable:

**Is this my story to tell?** Gayatri Spivak asked "Can the subaltern speak?" and the answer was essentially: not when Western intellectuals keep speaking for them. If I'm horrified by a practice, am I centering the people affected by it, or am I centering my own discomfort?

**Are the people within this culture fighting this fight already?** Usually, yes. And they don't need me, a Czech girl with a Substack and a pet frog, to lead the charge. Change that comes from within, using a culture's own moral language, tends to stick. Change imposed from outside tends to create backlash.

**Am I applying the same standards to my own culture?** Because if I'm not willing to examine pomlazka with the same critical eye I bring to the Wayuu encierro, then I'm not doing ethics. I'm doing tourism.

**Am I reducing a complex culture to its most shocking element?** A single practice is not a civilization. If the only thing you know about the Wayuu is the encierro, you don't know the Wayuu. You know one story. And a single story, as Adichie reminds us, is always incomplete. You don't know about the mochilas, or Walekeru the spider, or the fact that their communities are increasingly led by women, or that their desert is being eaten alive by a coal mine. You just know the one thing that made you feel something, and you mistook that feeling for understanding.

**Would I want someone making this judgment about me based on pomlazka alone?** If all you knew about Czech culture was that men whip women with sticks once a year, you'd think we were insane. And, well, maybe we are a little. But there's more to the story. There always is.

## the whip and the loom

I started with Czech Easter because I wanted to earn the right to talk about this. It felt dishonest to point at other cultures' uncomfortable practices without first sitting with my own. And honestly? Sitting with it was harder than I expected. I called my mom while writing this and asked her what she thought about pomlazka. She said she loved it as a girl, that it was exciting, that the whole village came alive. Then she paused and said, "But I also remember hiding in the bathroom when your uncle's friends got too drunk." She didn't elaborate. She didn't have to.

Pomlazka is dying in Czech cities (nobody rings my doorbell in Prague anymore), and maybe that's liberation, or maybe it's loss, or maybe it's both. The women in my grandmother's village who still participate in it aren't victims, and they aren't fools. They're people navigating the messy space between tradition and individual autonomy, same as everyone else.

The Wayuu women weaving mochilas in the desert of La Guajira are doing the same thing. So are the Muslim feminists reinterpreting scripture. So are the Roma communities fighting for recognition while trying to preserve what makes them who they are.

The real question was never "is this tradition barbaric?" That question is a dead end. It invites judgment without understanding, condemnation without curiosity, and a comfortable moral superiority that has historically been used to justify the worst things humans have done to each other.

The better question is: who benefits from this practice, and who suffers? Are the people who suffer able to leave, to resist, to change things from within? And what is my role, as an outsider who happened to wander into this story?

I don't know. Or maybe I do know, but I don't trust my own certainty enough to prescribe it. Because the moment I start handing out moral verdicts on cultures I've only glimpsed from the outside, I become part of a very old, very ugly tradition.

And I've already got one problematic tradition to deal with. The one with the willow whip and the slivovice.
