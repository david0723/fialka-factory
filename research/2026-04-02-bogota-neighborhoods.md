## Chapinero Alto

**How it differs from the rest of Chapinero**: Chapinero Alto is where the flat city streets meet the eastern hills. The upper streets around Carrera 4A and Calle 57 have a quieter, more bohemian feel than the commercial bustle of lower Chapinero. Small design studios, used-book stores, and vintage shops line steep residential streets with views over Bogota's skyline.

**Architecture**: Like other parts of Chapinero, you'll find the red-brick English-style houses designed under Austrian urban planner Karl Brunner's influence in the 1920s-30s. But up here the buildings tend to be smaller and more residential, mixed with mid-century apartment blocks. The hillside setting gives the area a distinct, almost European village character.

**What it's known for**: Specialty coffee culture and a growing food scene. Catacion Publica (part cafe, part coffee lab where local baristas train) is a neighborhood staple. Contemporary bistro El Chato, where chef Alvaro Clavijo showcases Colombian ingredients with international techniques, was named the fifth best restaurant in Latin America in 2022. The steep streets reward wandering with unexpected finds: indie bookshops, hole-in-the-wall empanada joints, and artisan bakeries.

**Nightlife**: The craft beer scene is strong here. Tierra Santa, El Mono Bandido, and Cerveceria Gigante are popular brewpubs. The famous Theatron, one of the largest nightclubs in Latin America with over a dozen themed rooms, is in the broader Chapinero area.

**Green spaces**: Quebrada La Vieja is a steep nature trail that starts in the neighborhood and climbs into the eastern hills with great city views. Parque de los Hippies is a relaxed green space good for people-watching.

## Calle de los Anticuarios

**What it is**: A steep, narrow street about 250 meters long in the El Nogal neighborhood (Calle 79B, between Carrera 7 and Carrera 9). Despite its name ("Antique Dealers' Street"), it has evolved well beyond antiques.

**History**: In the early 1990s, around 16 antique dealers set up shop on this block, making it a destination for collectors seeking pieces unavailable elsewhere in Bogota. The street was formerly part of a family estate connected to Colombian republican-era history. Its architectural centerpiece is the Chapel of Santa Maria de los Angeles, built in 1920, which belonged to the Holguin family.

**What it's like now**: Only three antique dealers remain. The historic mansions and chalets have been converted into exclusive Colombian design boutiques, art galleries, designer clothing stores, and interior decoration shops. The elegant brick houses create a charming backdrop. The street has become a hub for Bogota's fashion and design scene, even hosting events during Bogota Fashion Week.

**Why visit**: It's a window into how Bogota's wealthy neighborhoods evolve. You go for the curated mix of Colombian craftsmanship, contemporary art, and the occasional genuine antique find. The architecture alone is worth the walk.

## Usaquen

**Overview**: A charming neighborhood in northern Bogota that feels distinctly separate from the rest of the city. Usaquen was an independent village in Cundinamarca until it was absorbed into Bogota in 1954, and it retains that small-town atmosphere around its colonial plaza.

**Architecture**: Spanish colonial style, similar to La Candelaria but better preserved and less chaotic. The colonial quarter features the Santa Barbara church (originally constructed in 1665, modernized in the 20th century) and whitewashed buildings around a central plaza. The surrounding streets mix colonial structures with upscale modern restaurants and boutiques.

**The flea market**: The Mercado de las Pulgas de Usaquen takes over the streets every Sunday. Local artisans sell handcrafted jewelry, leather goods, textiles, ceramics, and antiques. Street food vendors and live musicians complete the atmosphere. It's touristy but genuinely enjoyable and a good place to buy quality Colombian handicrafts.

**Beyond the market**: During the week, Usaquen is a quieter, upscale dining destination. The cobblestone streets around the plaza are lined with restaurants ranging from high-end Colombian cuisine to international options. The neighborhood is considered one of the safest in Bogota with good pedestrian infrastructure.

**How it differs from other zones**: If Candelaria is the gritty historic center and Chapinero is the creative urban core, Usaquen is the polished, relaxed village escape. It's where Bogotanos go for a Sunday stroll that feels like leaving the city without actually leaving.

## Teusaquillo

**Overview**: A residential neighborhood southwest of Chapinero that most tourists overlook entirely. This is where Bogota's architectural diversity really shows. Teusaquillo has no malls, no Zona T-style shopping strips, and relatively few tourists. What it has is some of the most beautiful residential architecture in the city and a genuine neighborhood feel.

**Architecture**: In 1927, Teusaquillo was the most elegant residential sector in Bogota. Architects including Karl Brunner (again), Alberto Manrique Martin, and Galindo Guerra built houses blending English Tudor style with Art Deco touches. The result is streets of ornate early-20th-century homes that look nothing like the colonial center or the modern north. Republican-era and Art Deco buildings line tree-shaded streets.

**What to do**: ParkWay is the neighborhood's signature landmark, a one-block-wide, 800-meter-long linear park designed by Karl Brunner in 1950. Locals walk their dogs, exercise, eat at the surrounding restaurants, and hang out on the grass. It's a perfect spot for people-watching Bogotano daily life.

The National Museum of Colombia is here, the oldest and largest museum in the country with over 20,000 pieces spanning pre-Columbian artifacts to contemporary art. Casa E Borrero hosts contemporary theater, dance, and experimental art.

**Cafe culture**: Quipile Cafe de Origen is a minimalist specialty cafe where baristas walk you through the differences between single-origin Colombian beans from different regions.

**Nearby**: La Macarena, a bohemian pocket known for street art, international restaurants (Argentinian empanadas, Spanish tapas, Peruvian cuisine alongside Colombian food), and a laidback artsy vibe. Worth a detour but exercise normal caution at night.

**Why visit**: Teusaquillo is the antidote to tourist-district Bogota. The architecture alone justifies a morning walk, and the neighborhood gives you a sense of how middle-class Bogotanos actually live. If you want to understand the city beyond the guidebook zones, this is where to go.

## Parque 93

**Overview**: An upscale neighborhood in northern Chapinero centered around its namesake park (Parque de la 93), a leafy green space with temporary art exhibits, a water feature, and Andes mountain views in the background. The area around the park is one of Bogota's premier dining and nightlife districts.

**Vibe**: More polished and cosmopolitan than Zona G, less commercial than Zona T. The park itself is a gathering point surrounded by outdoor terraces. On weekends, families and couples fill the benches while the restaurants buzz with brunch crowds.

**Dining**: The culinary scene leans high-end but with character. Amor Perfecto was one of the first specialty coffee spots in Bogota and has trained many of the city's best baristas. Azahar Cafe 93 grows its own small-plot beans. Gaira Cafe, owned by Grammy-winning singer Carlos Vives' family, combines Colombian cuisine with live music and has its own concert auditorium. For something different, The Pot Asian is a fully vegan bowl spot, fresh and filling. Galeria Cafe Libro has hosted comedy and salsa shows for over forty years.

**Safety**: The Chico / Chapinero area around Parque 93 is among the safest zones for visitors, with strong lighting, active street life, and high pedestrian traffic. A good base if you want to be in a walkable, restaurant-rich area without the edge of Candelaria.

**How it differs from other zones**: Where Zona G is specifically about fine dining and Zona T is about shopping, Parque 93 balances dining, nightlife, green space, and cultural events around a single park. It's the most well-rounded of the upscale Chapinero sub-zones.

## Additional Neighborhood Worth Considering

### Quinta Camacho

**Why include it**: A small, distinct sub-neighborhood within Chapinero that sits between Chapinero Alto and Zona T. Many travel writers recommend it as the single best area for tourists to base themselves in Bogota.

**Architecture**: Built in the 1930s as planned urbanization for wealthy Bogotanos, Quinta Camacho combines English and Parisian architectural styles. Tree-lined streets with ornate early-20th-century houses that have been converted into restaurants, cafes, bars, and boutique hotels.

**What it's like**: Walkable, safe, and centrally located. Bohemian cafes and trendy boutiques sit alongside nightclubs and diverse restaurants. It blends historic charm with a contemporary edge, making it feel like a self-contained village within the larger Chapinero district.

**Why it matters for the guide**: If Karolina is recommending where to stay or where to base yourself, Quinta Camacho is the neighborhood that keeps coming up in traveler recommendations. It's safe, has excellent dining, and is well-connected to the rest of the city.
