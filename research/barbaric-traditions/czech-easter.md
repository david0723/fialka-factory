## 1. Historical and Pagan Origins

The pomlázka tradition dates back to at least the 14th century, with written documents referencing it from the 1300s. It almost certainly predates Christianity's arrival in the Czech lands, making it a pagan fertility ritual that was later absorbed into Easter celebrations.

The word *pomlázka* comes from the Czech verb *pomladit*, meaning "to make younger" — the core belief being that whipping with fresh willow branches transfers the vitality of spring to the person being whipped, ensuring their health, youth, and fertility for the coming year.

The tradition closely resembles other pre-Christian fertility rites, most notably the Roman **Lupercalia**, where semi-naked young men whipped women (and the soil) with leather thongs to promote prosperity and fertility. This parallel suggests a shared Indo-European ritual lineage rather than a uniquely Czech invention.

**How it became "Easter":** As Christianity spread through Central Europe, the practice was "Christianized" and anchored to Easter Monday — though it has no connection to the biblical Easter narrative. Czech church officials have noted the custom is folk-based, not religiously mandated.

## 2. How the Tradition Works in Practice

On Easter Monday, boys and men go from house to house carrying a *pomlázka* — a whip braided from 4 to 24 fresh willow (pussy willow) twigs, decorated with colorful ribbons. Each ribbon is typically added by a different girl/woman visited throughout the day; a heavily ribboned whip is a point of pride.

The ritual encounter lasts roughly 20 seconds: the man recites a traditional Easter carol (a rhyme asking for eggs), then lightly whips the woman on her legs or backside. She responds with "thank you" and gives him a painted egg (*kraslice*), candy, or (increasingly) a shot of alcohol.

Women traditionally wear multiple long dresses or skirts to cushion the impact. If men arrive after midday, women can retaliate by throwing a bucket of cold water on them — the "you're late" penalty.

**Regional variations:**
- **Moravia**: Alcohol (shots of slivovice) plays a bigger role than in Bohemia; the whole day becomes a village-wide social event with food, drink, and visiting
- **Some Moravian villages**: Practice an egalitarian reversal on Easter Tuesday where women can douse or whip men back
- **Slovakia**: Similar tradition called *šibačka* with the whip called *korbáč*
- **Urban areas**: The tradition is largely extinct in Prague and other big cities; survives mainly in villages and small towns

## 3. The Current Discourse

### 3a. Defenders' Arguments

- **Cultural identity**: Supporters frame pomlázka as essential Czech cultural heritage, a thread connecting present-day Czechs to their ancestors. Abandoning it would mean losing a piece of national identity.
- **Harmless fun**: Proponents say the whipping is symbolic — "mostly gentle pats," not actual violence. Nobody gets hurt.
- **Community building**: Especially in villages, Easter Monday is one of the few remaining occasions for neighbors to visit each other's homes, share food and drink, and socialize across generations.
- **60% support**: A 2019 survey found that 60% of Czech households still follow the tradition of spanking or watering someone on Easter Monday.
- **Women enjoy it too**: Some women report positive memories — being chased around by uncles and cousins, the excitement of the day.

### 3b. Critics' Arguments

**Feminist and consent-based criticism:**
- The tradition is inherently gendered and one-directional: men are active aggressors, women are passive recipients
- Women face social pressure to participate even when they don't want to, undermining genuine consent
- It normalizes physical contact that women cannot refuse without social consequences
- Liberal theologians and feminist religious circles have condemned its "patriarchal spirit" and promotion of gendered violence

**Survey data (NMS Market Research, ~1,000 respondents, cited in Expats.cz and Brno Daily):**
- **80% of Czech women** dislike the tradition
- **31%** of women actively avoid participation
- **44%** report experiencing pain
- **25%** report getting bruises
- **19%** report feelings of helplessness or humiliation
- **15%** feel humiliated and afraid (2019 Behavio Labs survey)
- **10%** are brought to tears (2019 Behavio Labs survey)

**Alcohol as accelerant:** Up to one-third of the population identifies excessive alcohol consumption during Easter whipping as the most significant problem. Accounts of "drunk young men taking it too far" are common, particularly in villages.

**Notable quotes:**
- Czech woman Vanda: *"I actually like the tradition a lot... [but] culture around it is very problematic"*
- Negative experience: *"I used to hide in my room all day so that no-one would whip me"*
- Disturbing: *"It's the only time of year I can beat my wife without guilt"*
- Author's daughter: *"I really don't want to be whipped, even if it's light, but I also kind of do want to be whipped, because it's tradition"* — illustrating the internalized pressure

## 4. Evolution and Generational Shift

### Dying in cities, surviving in villages

The tradition is "substantially extinct in bigger cities" and persists mainly in rural areas where it serves as a community event and social glue. One respondent noted: *"Fifteen years ago I was meticulously making Easter eggs for people but now no one rings the doorbell anyway."*

### Generational divide

- **64% of Gen Z men** support the tradition (it's a "social event" — an excuse to visit friends and drink)
- **Only 26% of Gen Z women** support it
- Survey author Lucie Režná: *"Men from the youngest generations most often go out to carol singing and whipping; for them, it is mainly a social event"*

### Behavioral evolution

The Wikipedia article notes a significant behavioral shift: *"In the past, young boys would chase young girls on village streets with the whips... Playful running around still occurs, but aggressive ambushing is now considered unacceptable bullying by the modern generation."*

### Broader Easter trends

A Košík.cz survey of 1,350 Czechs found:
- **49%** of households treat Easter traditions as merely symbolic gestures
- Easter is increasingly about family time, nature, and spring rather than any specific customs
- Travel and countryside escapes are replacing traditional observances
- Only **9%** plan to attend church
- Food traditions (lamb cake, dyed eggs) persist more strongly than the whipping custom

## 6. Similar Traditions Elsewhere (for context)

- **Śmigus-dyngus** (Poland): Water-dousing tradition on Easter Monday, also with gendered dynamics
- **Šibačka** (Slovakia): Nearly identical whipping tradition with the *korbáč*
- **Siba** (Hungary): Similar whipping custom with the *korbács*
- **Croatia**: Olive twig version, though the whip is not actually used for striking
- **Lupercalia** (Ancient Rome): The likely ancestor — semi-naked young men whipping women and soil for fertility
