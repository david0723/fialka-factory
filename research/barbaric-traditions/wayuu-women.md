# Research Notes: Wayuu Tribe Traditions Regarding Women

## Overview

The Wayuu (also Wayúu) are the largest indigenous group in Colombia and Venezuela, numbering around 800,000 people. They inhabit the arid La Guajira Peninsula in northern Colombia and northwestern Venezuela. Their society is matrilineal — children carry their mother's last name, inheritance passes through the female line, and territorial divisions (rancherías) are named after the mother's clan. Women are the primary weavers, cultural transmitters, and increasingly, community leaders.

**Key sources**: [Wikipedia: Wayuu people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wayuu_people), [Lonely Planet: Colombia's most famous weavers](https://www.lonelyplanet.com/articles/the-wayuu-colombias-most-famous-weavers), [Upstream Journal: Wayuu women of La Guajira](https://upstreamjournal.org/wayuu-women-of-la-guajira/)

## 1. The Encierro (Enclosure/Confinement) Tradition

### What it is

The encierro is the central coming-of-age ritual in Wayuu culture. It begins when a girl experiences her first menstruation (menarche). She is secluded in a special space — either a separate hut or a partitioned area within the family home, divided from the rest by a white sheet or fence made of wood and cactus.

The Wayuu term for this rite of passage is **Süttüsü Paülü'ü**. Through it, the girl transitions from child to **Majayut** (Lady/Woman).

### Duration

Traditionally, the encierro lasted **one to five years** (measured in "moons" — 12 moons being one year). Wikipedia cites a range of "two months up to two years." Today, the duration varies widely depending on the family — from a few weeks or days to several months. Modernization has significantly shortened it for most families.

**Sources**: [Latin American Post: The first menstruation — Wayuu mysticism](https://latinamericanpost.com/31480-the-first-menstruation-wayuu-mysticism), [Lombia+Co: The meaning behind el encierro](https://www.lombia.co/the-meaning-behind-the-sacred-wayuu-ritual-of-el-encierro/)

### What happens during the encierro

**Physical elements:**
- The girl's **hair is cut short** (to ear length), which intentionally causes a sense of shame — a symbolic shedding of childhood
- She remains in a **hammock elevated from the floor**, standing only at night or when necessary
- She takes **early morning cold showers/baths** — intended to build "a strong character, so she's not going to smile to every man" (Estercilia Simanca, Wayuu writer)
- Her **skin pales** from lack of sunlight exposure — this is the origin of the term "blanqueo" (whitening)
- She follows a **strict diet**: only herbal beverages called "brebajes" are consumed; milk, meat, sugar, and salt are prohibited
- She loses weight from the dietary restrictions
- **Vomiting** during the process is interpreted as the "child's spirit" leaving her body
- She follows a **vegetarian diet** called Jaguapi

**Educational elements (what she learns):**
- **Weaving and crocheting** — the foundational skill of Wayuu womanhood. The Wayúu saying goes: "To be a woman is to know how to weave"
- **Cooking** and traditional medicine
- **Public behavior** and social conduct — when to speak and when to remain silent
- **Conflict mediation** skills — particularly family disputes
- **Cultural knowledge** — myths, values, spiritual practices, responsibilities of a wife and mother

**Social elements:**
- Contact is restricted to **female relatives only** — the girl's mother, grandmother, aunts serve as instructors
- Men are completely excluded from the space
- At the end of the encierro, the community **celebrates her emergence** as an adult woman, now eligible for marriage

**Sources**: [Latin American Post](https://latinamericanpost.com/31480-the-first-menstruation-wayuu-mysticism), [Lombia+Co: Weaving and womanhood](https://www.lombia.co/weaving-womanhood-wayuu-encierro/)

## 2. Weaving: More Than a Craft

Weaving is inseparable from the encierro — it is the primary skill taught during seclusion and the activity that defines Wayuu womanhood.

### Origin myth

According to Wayuu mythology, a spider called **Walekeru** taught the first Wayuu woman to weave. The craft is thus considered a sacred, ancestral gift.

### The patterns (Kanaasü)

Wayuu woven designs are called **Kanaasü** ("the art of weaving drawing"). They are intricate geometric patterns, each with a specific name and meaning, inspired by Wayuu cosmology, legends, and the natural landscape of La Guajira — the desert, animals, stars, and water.

### Cultural and economic significance

- Weaving is "a symbol of wisdom, creativity, intelligence and status"
- The **mochila** (crocheted bag) is the most iconic Wayuu product and "the most exported handicraft in all of Colombia"
- Selling handwoven creations has become a **primary income source** for Wayuu women, who are the main providers in their families
- Fair trade initiatives and global interest in indigenous craftsmanship have created economic opportunities that also preserve cultural practices
- Traditional natural dyes have largely been replaced by acrylic threads from commercial companies, producing brighter, more durable colors

**Sources**: [Lonely Planet](https://www.lonelyplanet.com/articles/the-wayuu-colombias-most-famous-weavers), [Textile Arts Center: History in Threads](https://textileartscenter.com/feature/history-in-threads-exploring-wayuu-mochilas/), [Hands of Colombia: Wayuu Mochila Bags](https://handsofcolombia.com/blogs/news/wayuu-mochila-bags-deep-dive)

## 3. Insider Perspectives: How Wayuu Women View Their Own Traditions

### The "don't judge from outside" framing

A crucial insider perspective comes from within the culture: **"This is a very important event in the life of the Wayúu people which should not be interpreted or judged from the perspective of the Alijuna [non-indigenous person], since within the Wayuú culture there is no attempt to give a woman commercial value, but rather a moral value."**

This directly addresses the outsider tendency to see the encierro as preparation for "selling" a bride. The Wayuu frame it as investing moral and cultural worth in women.

**Source**: [Lombia+Co: El Encierro](https://www.lombia.co/the-meaning-behind-the-sacred-wayuu-ritual-of-el-encierro/)

### Estercilia Simanca Pushaina — the Wayuu writer who didn't go through encierro

Estercilia Simanca (b. 1975) is one of the most prominent Wayuu voices in Colombian literature. She is a writer, attorney, and cultural researcher from the Caicemapa reservation. Her novella **"El encierro de una pequeña doncella"** (The Enclosure of a Young Maiden, 2003) was included in the 2006 International Board on Books for Young People (IBBY) Honor List.

Key insights from Simanca:
- She clarifies: **"The 'encierro' is not confinement, it's a room (or rancho, as we say) for the girl"** — pushing back against the Western "imprisonment" framing
- She herself **did not go through the encierro** — and notes that a Wayuu woman who skips it is called a **"venado" (deer)** — "eternally infantile and rebellious, a woman Irama"
- She acknowledges that had she undergone the ritual, **"surely her voice and her words would be different, as she would have learned to keep quiet when the time was right"** — a strikingly self-aware observation about how the ritual shapes (and constrains) women's expression
- Despite this, she resists being categorized as an "indigenous writer," preferring to be seen simply as a Colombian or Latin American writer

Simanca occupies a fascinating position: she is a Wayuu woman who both defends the cultural meaning of the encierro and is herself an example of what happens when a woman breaks from it — she became an outspoken writer and lawyer, arguably because she wasn't taught to "keep quiet."

**Sources**: [Latin American Literature Today: Estercilia Simanca](https://latinamericanliteraturetoday.org/2018/07/estercilia-simanca-writer-who-makes-desert-blossom-ana-maria-ferreira/), [Library of Congress: Simanca reading](https://www.loc.gov/item/2023712817/), [Providence Digital Commons: El encierro de una pequeña doncella](https://digitalcommons.providence.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=2383&context=inti)

### Karen Tiller Pana — community leader

Karen Tiller Pana, a Wayuu community leader, speaks about the tension between preservation and modernization: **"Modernization [is] causing a loss of interest in the ancestral traditions among the younger generations."** She also emphasizes the Wayuu view of nature as interconnected, noting that even in their harsh desert, "if a plant survives this, it is a very great accomplishment and it is an immense joy, which we must take care of."

**Source**: [Upstream Journal](https://upstreamjournal.org/wayuu-women-of-la-guajira/)

## 4. Outsider Reactions and Western Reporting

### The "shock" framing

Most Western coverage of the encierro leads with elements designed to provoke outsider discomfort: isolation, food deprivation, skin whitening, weight loss. Words like "locked up," "confined," and "imprisoned" appear frequently. The physical transformation (paling skin, weight loss, hair cutting) maps easily onto Western frameworks of control and bodily harm.

### What gets lost

What these accounts typically underplay:
- The **matrilineal context** — this happens in a society where women hold significant power, own property through clan lines, and are primary economic providers
- The **educational dimension** — the encierro is essentially a years-long intensive mentorship by elder women, covering everything from conflict mediation to economic skills (weaving)
- The **community celebration** at the end — the girl emerges with elevated status, not diminished
- The **consent and family context** — this is a family-led process, not an institutional one

### Academic access challenges

Researchers note that it "can sometimes take years for outside researchers and anthropologists to earn the trust of indigenous people and to develop the intercultural skills needed to gain insights about indigenous cultures and perspectives." As Colombian national laws have contradicted traditional uses, some Wayuu have become more cautious about sharing customs with outsiders.

**Source**: [Vanderbilt: Gender relations and the Wayúu](https://mzo.library.vanderbilt.edu/etnografico/gender)

## 5. Is the Tradition Changing?

### Shortened duration

The most visible change: duration has collapsed. Where it once lasted 1-5 years, many families now practice a symbolic version lasting days or weeks. Some families skip it entirely.

### Governance transformation

In 2005, male elders in one Wayuu community appointed **Neris Uriana** as their first female chieftain. The reasoning was pragmatic: they believed women's "more peace-loving and more humanitarian" approach would resolve inter-tribal conflicts better. After her success, **the community permanently committed to appointing only women leaders.** Men now share domestic responsibilities, and women have expanded opportunities to work and lead.

**Source**: [The World from PRX: This indigenous tribe is run solely by women](https://theworld.org/stories/2018/04/24/indigenous-tribe-run-solely-women)

### Economic pressures and mining

The El Cerrejón coal mine — one of the largest open-pit mines in the world — operates in Wayuu territory. Many Wayuu, including women, work in the mine despite environmental devastation. Mining prevents traditional agriculture and hunting, creating an impossible choice: economic survival vs. cultural preservation.

The **Fuerza de Mujeres Wayuu** (Force of Wayuu Women), founded in 2006, denounces human rights violations from mining mega-projects, forced displacement, and armed conflict.

**Sources**: [Cultural Survival: Extracted from Colombia](https://www.culturalsurvival.org/publications/cultural-survival-quarterly/extracted-colombia-one-womans-work-counteract-destructive), [Mongabay: Threatened women of the Wayuú community](https://news.mongabay.com/2021/12/in-colombia-threatened-women-of-the-wayuu-community-continue-to-fight-rampant-mining/), [Forest Peoples Programme: Fuerza de Mujeres Wayuu](https://www.forestpeoples.org/en/partner/fuerza-de-mujeres-wayuu)

### Generational tension

Younger Wayuu are increasingly disinterested in ancestral traditions. Globalization, technology, and urbanization pull youth away from the ranchería lifestyle. Yet the economic success of Wayuu weaving — driven by global demand for mochilas — creates a counterforce: tradition as livelihood.

## 6. Key Tensions for the Article

These are the tensions most relevant to fialka's "barbaric traditions" piece:

1. **Outsider horror vs. insider meaning**: The encierro looks like imprisonment to Western eyes. To Wayuu women, it's a prestigious rite that confers adult status, economic skills, and cultural knowledge. Neither perspective is the full picture.

2. **Matriarchy complicates the narrative**: This isn't a patriarchal society isolating women. Women control inheritance, lead clans, and increasingly govern communities. The encierro exists within a framework of female power, not male control.

3. **The Estercilia paradox**: A Wayuu woman who skipped the encierro became an internationally recognized writer — partly because she wasn't taught silence. She defends the tradition's cultural meaning while embodying what happens when women break from it.

4. **Consent is contextual**: There's no individual opt-in for a 12-year-old. But framing it as "forced" ignores that Western coming-of-age is also heavily structured (school, legal drinking ages, driving ages). The question is which structures we normalize and which we condemn.

5. **Tradition vs. survival**: The real existential threat to Wayuu culture isn't the encierro — it's coal mining, forced displacement, water scarcity, and government neglect. Western concern about the encierro while ignoring these material threats reveals something about whose discomfort matters.

6. **The "blanqueo" optics**: The skin-whitening effect is incidental (caused by sun deprivation), but the term "blanqueo" and the stated goal that the girl "gets prettier" are uncomfortable in any context. This is worth engaging honestly rather than explaining away.
