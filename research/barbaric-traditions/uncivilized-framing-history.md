**Date**: 2026-04-03

## 1. Native Americans: "Merciless Indian Savages"

### The Framing

From the earliest colonial encounters, Native Americans were cast as "savages" — a word that carried connotations of demonic origin, moral depravity, and subhuman status. This wasn't casual prejudice; it was a systematic rhetorical framework designed to legitimize dispossession.

- **The Declaration of Independence** itself memorializes Native Americans as "merciless Indian savages" — racist language enshrined in the founding document of a nation built on their land. [Source: Mackay & Feagin, "Merciless Indian Savages": Deconstructing Anti-Indigenous Framing](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/23326492221112040)
- **Pilgrims and natural hierarchy**: Early colonists viewed Indigenous peoples as inferior based on a Eurocentric "natural law" philosophy — a God-designed hierarchy placing white Europeans at the top. Their lack of European-style weapons, agriculture, and governance was read as proof of primitiveness. [Source: The American Yawp, Ch. 2: Colliding Cultures](https://www.americanyawp.com/text/02-colliding-cultures/)
- **The "savage" as threat and victim simultaneously**: White violence against Indigenous peoples was framed as "virtuous Christian self-defense," while Indigenous resistance was framed as savage aggression. Europeans were always the victims, never the invaders. [Source: Mackay & Feagin](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/23326492221112040)

### How It Justified Genocide

- **Thomas Jefferson** initially believed Indigenous peoples could assimilate into white society. When he decided assimilation was impossible, he advocated extermination or displacement. The framing of "savagery" made this shift from assimilation to genocide feel like a natural, rational progression. [Source: HISTORY — When Native Americans Were Slaughtered in the Name of 'Civilization'](https://www.history.com/articles/native-americans-genocide-united-states)
- **The Indian Removal Act (1830)** formalized displacement, leading to the Trail of Tears. The rhetorical groundwork — decades of "savage" framing — made forced marches and mass death politically palatable. [Source: UAB Institute for Human Rights](https://sites.uab.edu/humanrights/2017/04/17/indian-removal-act-genocide-native-americans/)
- The genocidal destruction of Native Americans was "rationalized and virtuized due to alleged Indigenous savagery" — the framing didn't just accompany the violence, it *enabled* it. [Source: Mackay & Feagin](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/23326492221112040)

### Modern Persistence

The "savage" framing echoes in modern discourse: sports mascots, Hollywood stereotypes, and the ongoing erasure of Indigenous perspectives from mainstream narratives about American history.

## 2. Indigenous Peoples of South America: Conquest as Spiritual Duty

### The Framing

Spanish and Portuguese colonizers used a combination of religious doctrine, Aristotelian philosophy, and papal authority to frame Indigenous South Americans as subhuman.

- **Columbus** saw Indigenous peoples as "primitive and beast-like" and immediately noted their lack of sophisticated weapons — not as a sign of peacefulness, but as proof they would make "ideal servants." [Source: Khan Academy — The Spanish Conquistadores](https://www.khanacademy.org/humanities/us-history/precontact-and-early-colonial-era/spanish-colonization/a/the-spanish-conquistadores-and-colonial-empire)
- **The "spiritual conquest"**: Queen Isabella saw territorial expansion and Catholic evangelization as inseparable. Converting "heathens" was both a religious duty and a political tool for extending sovereignty. [Source: Wikipedia — Spanish Colonization of the Americas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas)
- **Papal decree Inter caetera (1493)**: Pope Alexander VI literally divided the Western Hemisphere between Spain and Portugal, with the condition that they spread Christianity. God himself, through his earthly representative, authorized the conquest. [Source: Wikipedia — Spanish Colonization of the Americas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas)

### The Valladolid Debate (1550–1551): Europe Argues Whether Indigenous People Are Human

This is one of the most revealing episodes in colonial history — the first public European debate about the morality of conquering Indigenous peoples.

- **Juan Ginés de Sepúlveda** argued, drawing on Aristotle, that Indigenous peoples were "natural slaves" incapable of self-governance. He listed four justifications for "just war": (1) their inability to rule themselves, (2) Spanish duty to prevent cannibalism, (3) duty to stop human sacrifice, (4) duty to convert them to Christianity. [Source: History Today — The Valladolid Debate on the Rights of Indigenous People](https://www.historytoday.com/archive/months-past/valladolid-debate-rights-indigenous-people)
- **Bartolomé de las Casas** countered that Indigenous peoples were fully rational beings who should be brought to Christianity without force. He rejected the application of Aristotle's "natural slave" concept. [Source: Gilder Lehrman Institute — Bartolomé de Las Casas Debates the Subjugation of the Indians](https://www.gilderlehrman.org/history-resources/spotlight-primary-source/bartolome-de-las-casas-debates-subjugation-indians-1550)
- **The "just war" doctrine** permitted enslaving any native who resisted conversion or opposed colonial authority — framing resistance to invasion as a moral failing that justified enslavement. [Source: MEDLIFE — Colonialism's Impact on Indigenous Cultures in South America](https://www.medlifemovement.org/medlife-stories/global-topics/colonialisms-impact-on-indigenous-cultures-in-south-america/)

### Key Takeaway for the Article

The Valladolid Debate is extraordinary because it was held *in the middle of active colonization*. Europeans were debating whether their victims were human while the killing continued. Sepúlveda's arguments — the appeal to cultural practices (cannibalism, human sacrifice) as justification for conquest — are almost identical to arguments still used today when Western commentators point to specific cultural practices to justify dismissing entire civilizations.

## 3. Africa: The "Civilizing Mission"

### The Framing

The colonization of Africa was justified through the ideology of the *mission civilisatrice* — the belief that Europeans had a moral duty to "civilize" supposedly primitive African peoples.

- **The three C's: Civilization, Christianity, and Commerce** — this was the explicit philosophical framework for African colonization. Each element reinforced the others: Africans needed civilization (European culture), Christianity (to save their souls), and commerce (European economic systems). [Source: Emory University — The Philosophy of Colonialism](https://scholarblogs.emory.edu/violenceinafrica/sample-page/the-philosophy-of-colonialism-civilization-christianity-and-commerce/)
- **Racial hierarchy as divine order**: European powers claimed that "as Christian nations, they were duty bound to disseminate Western civilization to what they perceived as heathen, primitive cultures." The framing presented colonization not as exploitation but as charity. [Source: Wikipedia — Civilizing Mission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Civilizing_mission)
- **Suppression of local cultures**: Traditional African languages, religions, governance systems, and cultural practices were systematically denigrated or banned — reframed from "different" to "backward." [Source: Lumen Learning — The "Civilising Mission"](https://courses.lumenlearning.com/suny-worldhistory/chapter/27-3-3-the-civilising-mission/)

### Kipling's "The White Man's Burden" (1899)

Rudyard Kipling's poem is the most concentrated expression of the civilizing mission ideology:

- Published to encourage the U.S. to colonize the Philippines, it frames imperialism as a "burden" — a selfless, thankless duty imposed on the white race by divine destiny.
- Filipinos (and by extension all colonized peoples) are described as "new-caught, sullen peoples, half-devil and half-child" — simultaneously dangerous and infantile.
- **Immediate backlash**: Henry Labouchère published *The Brown Man's Burden* (1899), a biting parody arguing the real "burden" was borne by colonized peoples. Mark Twain published *To the Person Sitting in Darkness* (1901), attacking the hypocrisy of the civilizing mission.
- [Source: Wikipedia — The White Man's Burden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_White_Man's_Burden)

### Key Takeaway for the Article

The civilizing mission is the template for "we know better than you" rhetoric that persists today. When Western commentators frame non-Western cultures as "behind" on a developmental timeline — as if all societies are on the same path, just at different stages — they're echoing the same logic that justified carving up Africa.

## 4. Roma People: Europe's Perpetual "Other"

### The Framing

The Roma (often pejoratively called "Gypsies") have faced continuous persecution since arriving in Europe in the 14th century, framed as inherently criminal, uncivilized, and incompatible with European society.

- **Racial and cultural othering**: Roma were viewed as intruders and threats, partly because of their dark skin (which Europeans "associated with ugliness, primitivism and Black People"), their perceived connection to the Muslim Ottoman Empire, and their distinct cultural practices. [Source: National WWII Museum — Fascination and Hatred: The Roma in European Culture](https://www.nationalww2museum.org/war/articles/roma-european-culture)
- **Stigmatized as criminals**: Roma were branded as "habitual criminals, social misfits, and vagabonds" — their nomadic lifestyle and close-knit communities read as evidence of deviance rather than cultural tradition. [Source: Cultural Survival — Persecution and Politicization](https://www.culturalsurvival.org/publications/cultural-survival-quarterly/persecution-and-politicization-roma-gypsies-eastern-europe)
- **The Holy Roman Empire (16th century)** banned Roma entirely, and *any citizen was allowed and encouraged to murder any Roma individual they encountered*. Bounties were paid for their capture, dead or alive. [Source: The Borgen Project — European Roma Persecution](https://borgenproject.org/european-roma-persecution/)

### Forced Assimilation

- **Austro-Hungarian Empire (18th century)**: Under Maria Theresa and Joseph II, Romani language, music, dress, and nomadism were outlawed. Traditional occupations were banned. The goal: erase Roma culture entirely.
- **Bulgaria (1960s)**: The government banned Roma culture including language, traditional music and dance, and Muslim religious practices.
- Children were forcibly removed from Roma families across multiple European countries throughout history.
- [Source: Council of Europe — History and Holocaust: Roma and Travellers](https://www.coe.int/en/web/roma-and-travellers/roma-history-/-holocaust)

### The Porajmos (Roma Holocaust)

Between 250,000 and 500,000 Roma were murdered by the Nazis and their collaborators during WWII. It was not until the 1980s–1990s that Roma were officially recognized as victims of racial persecution — *decades* after the war ended. Three concentration camp survivors had to go on hunger strikes to secure this recognition. [Source: Wikipedia — Romani Holocaust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romani_Holocaust)

### Key Takeaway for the Article

The Roma case is especially relevant because it's *ongoing* and *European*. While Westerners often point to "barbaric" practices in distant cultures, anti-Roma discrimination — including forced sterilization programs that continued into the 21st century in some European countries — represents a living example of how "uncivilized" framing persists in the heart of "civilized" Europe.

## 5. Jewish People: The "Enemy Within"

### The Framing

Jewish people were subjected to centuries of dehumanization in European Christian society, framed not as "primitive savages" (the colonial model) but as a dangerous, sinister internal enemy.

- **Blood libel**: The antisemitic myth that Jews murdered Christian children to use their blood in religious rituals (especially to bake Passover matzah). First documented case: William of Norwich, 1144. Blood libels led directly to pogroms and mass murder across Europe for centuries. [Source: US Holocaust Memorial Museum — Blood Libel](https://encyclopedia.ushmm.org/content/en/article/blood-libel)
- **Well-poisoning accusations**: During the Black Death (14th century), Jews were blamed for deliberately poisoning water wells to kill Christians — *despite Jews dying from the plague at the same rates*. In Strasbourg in February 1349, some 2,000 Jews were burned alive *before the plague even reached the city*. [Source: The Holocaust Explained — Medieval Antisemitism](https://www.theholocaustexplained.org/anti-semitism/medieval-antisemitism/)
- **Demonic imagery**: Christian art depicted Jews with horns, cloven feet, tails, and goat's beards. Jewish people were framed as "agents of the devil" — not just different, but actively evil. [Source: World Jewish Congress — Antisemitism Defined: Dehumanization, Demonization, and Stereotypes](https://www.worldjewishcongress.org/en/news/antisemitism-defined-dehumanization-demonization-and-stereotypes)
- **Ghettoization and legal othering**: Jews were concentrated in ghettos, banned from owning land, forced to swear special oaths, subjected to discriminatory taxes, and required to wear identifying clothing. [Source: Wikipedia — Medieval Antisemitism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval_antisemitism)

### How Jewish Othering Differs from Colonial Othering

Jewish people weren't framed as "primitive" or "uncivilized" — they were framed as *dangerously civilized*: cunning, conspiratorial, powerful behind the scenes. This is a different but parallel dehumanization pattern. Where colonial rhetoric says "they are too primitive to govern themselves," antisemitic rhetoric says "they are too powerful and must be contained." Both serve the same function: justifying exclusion, violence, and ultimately genocide.

### Modern Persistence

Blood libel themes resurface in modern antisemitic discourse, adapted to contemporary contexts. The underlying pattern — accusing a minority group of monstrous, hidden practices to justify their persecution — is remarkably durable. [Source: Berkeley Center for Jewish Studies — Modern Permutations of an Ancient Antisemitic Myth](https://jewishstudies.berkeley.edu/news/modern-permutations-ancient-antisemitic-myth-blood-libel-during-holocaust)

## 6. Common Rhetorical Patterns Across Cases

### The Shared Playbook

Despite targeting vastly different peoples across different centuries, the rhetoric of "uncivilized" framing follows remarkably consistent patterns:

**Pattern 1: Dehumanization through language**
- Native Americans: "savages," "merciless," "half-human"
- Indigenous South Americans: "primitive," "beast-like," "natural slaves"
- Africans: "heathen," "primitive," "half-devil and half-child"
- Roma: "vagabonds," "criminals," "social misfits"
- Jews: "agents of the devil," "poisoners," "Christ-killers"

The specific words change, but they all serve the same function: removing the target group from the category of "fully human," which makes violence against them permissible. [Source: Open University of Minnesota — Chapter 16: Settler-Colonial Rhetoric(s)](https://open.lib.umn.edu/rhetoricaltheory/chapter/the-settler-situation/)

**Pattern 2: Specific cultural practices cited as proof of barbarism**
- Cannibalism and human sacrifice (Indigenous Americas)
- "Heathen" religious practices (Africa)
- Nomadism and communal living (Roma)
- Religious dietary laws and rituals (Jews)
- Any practice that deviates from European Christian norms becomes "evidence" of inferiority

**Pattern 3: The civilizer-as-victim**
- Europeans were always framed as acting in self-defense, fulfilling divine duty, or bearing a thankless "burden." The actual victims — displaced, enslaved, murdered peoples — were cast as the aggressors or ingrates.
- White violence = virtuous self-defense / civilizing duty
- Indigenous resistance = savage aggression / ingratitude

**Pattern 4: The noble savage vs. the brutal savage (dual framing)**
- When colonizers wanted to assimilate Indigenous peoples, they deployed the "noble savage" myth — the idea of a pure, innocent primitive who could be uplifted.
- When colonizers wanted to exterminate, they deployed the "brutal savage" — irredeemably violent and dangerous.
- Both framings deny agency and full humanity. As scholars note: "Both the noble and the brutal savage are fantasies of the European mind that kept Indigenous peoples in a suspended state of either elevated purity or perpetual evil."
- [Source: The Conversation — Explainer: The Myth of the Noble Savage](https://theconversation.com/explainer-the-myth-of-the-noble-savage-55316)

**Pattern 5: Legal and institutional codification**
- The framing always moved from rhetoric to law: Indian Removal Act, papal decrees, anti-Roma edicts, anti-Jewish ghettoization laws, apartheid legislation. Once a group is successfully framed as "less than," legal systems formalize the discrimination.

**Pattern 6: The pattern persists in modern discourse**
- "They're not ready for democracy" (about Middle Eastern countries)
- "Their culture is incompatible with Western values" (about Muslim immigrants)
- "They refuse to integrate" (about Roma communities in Europe)
- "They have barbaric practices" (about any culture that doesn't conform to Western norms)

The specific targets rotate, but the rhetorical structure is the same one used to justify colonization: identify a cultural difference → frame it as evidence of inferiority → use that framing to justify control, exclusion, or violence.
